The missing layer

Archives hold fragments. Historical explanation depends on the links between them.

Event datasets can catalogue thousands of occurrences. Close reading can reconstruct a causal chain with great care. Between them sits a costly research problem: discovering which fragments may belong together.

A person can be named differently across scripts. An organization can change titles. A date can be approximate. Evidence of a relationship can be scattered across documents that no single researcher would normally read side by side.

This project explores a careful middle layer: use machines to widen the search for connections, then give researchers the evidence and uncertainty they need to judge each one.

What the prototype surfaced

One person became a bridge between two bodies of material.

In an early Hebrew–English test, the pipeline reconciled mentions of Orde Wingate and the Special Night Squads across languages. That identity link connected an Israeli special-forces lineage to material about the British wartime special-forces ecosystem.

Research principles

Scale without overclaiming.

01

Evidence before elegance

Every extracted item retains the wording that supports it, even when that makes the data messier.

02

Suggestion, not verdict

Identity matches and possible relationships are candidates for review, not facts smuggled in by automation.

03

Two kinds of uncertainty

Evidence that a connection exists is different from evidence that the connection caused a later event.

04

Independent sources matter

Three documents repeating one testimony should not count as three independent chains of evidence.
